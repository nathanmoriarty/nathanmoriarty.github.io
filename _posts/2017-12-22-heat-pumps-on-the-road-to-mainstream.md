class="c0"><span class="c16 c26">22 Dec 2017</span></p><p class="c0 c6"><span class="c26 c16"></span></p><p class="c0 c4"><span class="c5">Heat pumps could be our only hope of low carbon heat. There are only a couple of options to achieve this:</span></p><p class="c0 c9"><span class="c5">&nbsp;</span></p><p class="c0 c9"><span class="c11">Option A</span><span class="c5">: calls on energy giants to decarbonise gas, however:</span></p><p class="c0 c9"><span class="c5">&nbsp;</span></p><p class="c0 c8"><span class="c15">1.</span><span class="c21">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</span><span class="c5">Very strong price signals are needed to transition from natural gas</span></p><p class="c0 c8"><span class="c15">2.</span><span class="c21">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</span><span class="c5">Expertise is required from established companies who profit from status quo.</span></p><p class="c0 c6 c9"><span class="c5"></span></p><p class="c0 c9"><span class="c11">Option B</span><span class="c5">: calls upon us, as prosumers, to install heat pumps, however:</span></p><p class="c0 c9"><span class="c5">&nbsp;</span></p><p class="c0 c8"><span class="c15">1.</span><span class="c21">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</span><span class="c5">They are currently more expensive than conventional boilers due to installation</span></p><p class="c0 c8"><span class="c15">2.</span><span class="c21">&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</span><span class="c5">Households must be insulated to a high standard, have underfloor heating, and solar panels prior to thinking about one.</span></p><p class="c0 c9"><span class="c5">&nbsp;</span></p><p class="c0 c4"><span class="c5">But are we ready? Is there a catch ? and what exactly is a heat pump ?</span></p><p class="c0 c9"><span class="c5">&nbsp;</span></p><p class="c0 c9"><span class="c22 c39">In a recent survey Ipsos Mori found that 68% of householders had not heard of a heatpump.<br>&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;</span><span class="c21 c22">[</span><span class="c21 c22 c23">Ipsos Mori 2013</span><span class="c21 c22">]</span></p><p class="c0 c9"><span class="c5">&nbsp;</span></p><p class="c0"><span class="c7">The technical details</span></p><p class="c0"><span class="c5">&nbsp;</span></p><p class="c0 c4"><span class="c5">Heat pumps tap into natural thermal inertia from the surrounding environment and &lsquo;pump&rsquo; warmth into the house.</span></p><p class="c0 c9"><span class="c5">&nbsp;</span></p><p class="c0 c4"><span class="c5">This thermodynamic effect is a bit like a seesaw &ndash; you lean on one side and the other rises. This is measured with a factor called a COP &ndash; coefficient of performance:</span></p><p class="c0 c9"><span class="c5">&nbsp;</span></p><p class="c0 c9 c33"><span class="c11">COP</span><span class="c11 c13">heating</span><span class="c11">&nbsp;= T</span><span class="c11 c13">inside</span><span class="c11">&nbsp;/ (T</span><span class="c11 c13">inside</span><span class="c11">&nbsp;&ndash; T</span><span class="c11 c13">outside</span><span class="c11 c38">)</span></p><p class="c0 c9"><span class="c5">&nbsp;</span></p><p class="c0 c4"><span class="c5">This factor is the exciting part. Considering graph 1. At even as low as -10 (Deg C) outside you could have a COP of 20. This means 20 times more heat energy than you pay for in electrical energy !</span></p><p class="c0 c4"><span class="c5">&nbsp;</span></p><p class="c20 c4"></p></body><iframe src="https://drive.google.com/open?id=1CHYGosjhsOaQxoZrRicq7wHlMKeFKh5S/preview" width="400" height="480", align="centre"></iframe><body>
